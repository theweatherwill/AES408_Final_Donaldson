Began by figuring out which data source I wanted to use. First started with NASA Worldview,
however, that only gave me a GeoTiff with Red, Green, Blue bands, which would not be useful in
calculating NDVI or any other product that requires NIR or SWIR. Luckily, I found two events using
EarthData from NASA, two pristine events. The pristine events are what I will be starting with, to make
troubleshoot basic code simple. The data from EarthData contains SWIR and NIR, as well as other bands
that might not be helpful, but they're included! NIR and SWIR specifically will be helpful for my
hypothesis of NDVI or NBR, Normalized Burn Ratio, being useful.

In VSCode, I created a function for opening each band's data, since I wanted to open at least RGB, NIR,
and SWIR. I had the function open each tif file, reproject it, and then scale it for creating NDVI and
NBR. This is because NDVI and NBR are on a -1 to 1 scale, and raw bits or pixels does not calculate
properly. I opened the bands I needed for initial testing, then calculated NDVI and NBR. Plotting these
plots does not show a result I expected, as all my NDVI values appear as less than 0, which would
indicate water, but the test image is rural Kansas. I also had issues with my green and blue bands
before scaling, where they were identical to the red band or nonexistant. Going forward, I plan to
prioritize figuring out my issue with NDVI and NBR, then begin testing methods of identifying hail swaths.
I do not forsee any changes to my current proposal, though the hail swaths blend in more than I
expected with the other land features like croplands! So it'll be fun. My goal is to at least get
my code to detect the pristine swaths, though I am aiming for the code to identify a less-pristine
event as well. I will be using two events for each category of pristine, semi-pristine, and
non-pristine.
